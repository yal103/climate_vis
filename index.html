An explorable explanation by YangYang, Rocio, & Hoang

The world agreed on +2°C as a dangerous warming line. But that number is a global average. The Arctic crosses it long before the tropics; some regions hit it before today's high-schoolers retire. Scroll to see how a single global line becomes hundreds of local deadlines — and how much they shift when the world cuts emissions.

When does the world cross +2°C?

Under SSP2-4.5 — roughly today's policy trajectory — the map shows the first year each region's annual temperature climbs above +2°C relative to the 2015–2034 baseline. Some places are already there. Others won't cross within this century at all.

Hover any cell to see when warming arrives for that location.

Some regions cross decades before others.

The Arctic crosses +2°C roughly 30 years earlier than the tropical belt — a phenomenon called Arctic amplification. Land warms faster than ocean. Continental interiors warm faster than coasts. A single global threshold hides this huge spread of local deadlines.

Strong action buys years — but not equally.

This map shows years gained by mitigation: how much later each region crosses the threshold under SSP1-2.6 (strong mitigation) compared to SSP5-8.5 (high emissions). Darker green means a stronger payoff — sometimes decades of warming postponed. Gray regions cross under both scenarios, or escape under neither.

Which regions cross first?

A race chart makes the regional inequality unmistakable. Each bar is one named region; the marker is the year that region's area-weighted mean crosses the selected threshold. The Arctic always finishes first. The Antarctic and parts of the tropics may not cross at all.

Project Prototype Writeup

What we've done so far

We took our Project 3 dashboard and restructured it into a scroll-driven explorable explanation around a single thesis: 2°C is not one global deadline — it is many local deadlines. The page now opens with a narrative hero, then walks the reader through five guided steps before unlocking a fully interactive explore mode at the bottom. Each step is driven by an IntersectionObserver that updates the visualization state and swaps the visible chart in the sticky viz panel on the right.

We built two new visualizations the original dashboard didn't have. The years-gained mitigation map shows, per grid cell, how much later a region crosses the threshold under SSP1-2.6 versus SSP5-8.5. The regional race chart sorts ten named regions by the year they cross the selected threshold, making the inequality between Arctic, tropics, and Antarctic immediately legible. We also extended the cell time series chart so that selecting a location overlays the global mean line, giving an at-a-glance local-vs-global comparison.

What will be most challenging to design — and why

The hardest design problem is pacing — making the scroll feel like an article instead of a series of dashboard states. Each step needs enough text to land a point but not so much that the reader stops engaging with the visualization. We are still tuning how aggressively the controls reveal themselves: too early and the page feels like the old dashboard, too late and the reader cannot test the claim being made.

Annotations are the second hard problem. Calling out Arctic amplification or the tropical belt on the map means drawing labels and leader lines that survive across screen sizes and stay legible against a busy choropleth. We are still deciding whether to use static SVG annotations baked into the map, or animated annotations that fade in per scroll step. The mitigation map's diverging color scale is another challenge — encoding "years gained" alongside the categories "never crosses either" and "crosses under both" without overloading the legend is harder than it looked in the proposal.
