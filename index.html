Act I — The acceleration

Every year, warmer than the last.

Each stripe is a single year of projected global temperature. The cooler blues are the recent past; the angry reds are the futures we may inherit. From the top, the world's most ambitious mitigation path. From the bottom, fossil-fueled business as usual. The stripes don't lie — every scenario warms, the question is only how much.

Act II — The geography

But warming isn't uniform.

Some places will cross +2°C this decade. Others may not see it before 2100. The Arctic is already running ahead — high-latitude land warms two to four times the global average. Meanwhile vast stretches of the Southern Ocean stay cool, buffered by deep waters that absorb the heat we add.

Act III — The latitude divide

The Arctic races. The Southern Ocean stalls.

Each ridge below shows the distribution of crossing years for one latitude band. The Arctic peak crashes in early — most Arctic cells cross +2°C in the 2030s. The tropics are a broader, later wave. And the Southern Ocean barely rises off the floor: most of its area never crosses within this century at all.

Act IV — A patch of Earth, a single dot

Six thousand pieces of the planet.

Each dot is one grid cell — one patch of Earth's surface. Their horizontal position is the year they first cross +2°C. The dense red wall on the left is the Arctic. The blue cluster on the far right — beyond the timeline — are the cells that may never see +2°C. Together they tell the same story the map does, but stripped of geography.

Act V — The choice ahead

Three futures, branching from now.

Up to today, all three scenarios share the same line. After that, they fan apart — and the gap at 2100 is the difference our choices make. The shaded band between SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 is the possibility space: nearly 3°C of warming hangs on what humanity does this decade.

Act V — In a lifetime

What does this mean for you?

Type your birth year above. The timeline charts your life against the warming milestones that will mark it: when the Arctic crosses +2°C, when the world-as-a-whole does, when Europe does. These aren't abstractions — they're events your generation will live through. The scenario you pick changes the timing by decades.

Project writeup

What we've done so far

We took our Project 3 dashboard and rebuilt it from scratch as a scroll-driven story called A World on Fire. Instead of dumping everything on one page, the reader now scrolls through six acts, each with a different chart: warming stripes, an annotated world map, a ridge plot showing when different latitudes cross +2°C, a beeswarm where every dot is a grid cell, a fan chart of how the three SSP scenarios split apart, and finally a timeline you can personalize with your own birth year. We wanted at least half the visuals to be something other than a map, so the ridge plot and beeswarm were new territory for us.

On the code side, each visualization is its own D3 module, and we use Scrollama to detect which text block the reader is on and swap the active chart accordingly. The original Project 3 dashboard (map, line charts, histogram, year scrubber) still lives at the bottom so people can poke around after the narrative ends. Everything pulls from the same CMIP6 CESM2 data we processed in project-3.ipynb, so no new datasets were needed.

What will be the most challenging part

Honestly, the biggest challenge is making six totally different chart types feel like they belong together. Going from color-coded stripes to a density ridge plot to a force-layout beeswarm is a lot of context-switching for the reader. We're relying on a shared color palette across latitude bands and careful narration text to tie everything together, but it's tricky to get right and we're still tweaking the transitions between panels.

The beeswarm is also a real performance headache. There are about 6,000 dots and we run a d3-force collision simulation to lay them out, so we had to pre-settle it off-screen (140 ticks before painting anything) to avoid locking up the browser. It still has to re-run whenever you switch scenarios. Beyond that, the lifetime timeline at the end needs to land emotionally without being cluttered, and the whole thing has to work on phones where our two-column sticky layout has to collapse into a single column. Lots of small problems that add up.
